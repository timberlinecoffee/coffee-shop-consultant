# Module 3: Site Selection & Lease

**One-sentence description:** Find the right location for your coffee shop and sign a lease you can actually live with.

**Estimated completion time:** 2.5 to 3.5 hours across 5 to 7 sessions

---

## Who This Module Is For

You've finished Module 1 (you know your concept and your customer) and Module 2 (you've run your numbers). Now you need a real address. That's what this module is about.

Location is the decision that most new owners underestimate. Not because they pick bad streets, but because they don't know what questions to ask before they sign. This module teaches you the framework to evaluate any space and negotiate from a position of knowledge.

---

## Learning Objectives

By the end of this module, you will be able to:

- Explain what actually drives a coffee shop location decision beyond gut feel
- Read foot traffic patterns and daytime population data for any address
- Walk through a physical space and know what you're looking at
- Understand the 10 lease terms that matter most
- Know when to walk away from a deal
- Draft or review a Letter of Intent (LOI)
- Decide whether to hire a tenant rep broker (and how to find one)

---

## Module 3 Lessons

### Lesson 1: The Location Decision Framework

**What it covers:** The mental model you need before you look at a single space. Coffee shops fail for a lot of reasons, but a bad location is the hardest to recover from. This lesson gives you a structured way to evaluate any address against your concept from Module 1.

**Why it matters:** Most owners fall in love with a space before they've checked whether it can support their business. This lesson puts the filter in place first.

**Exercise:** Score three potential locations (real or hypothetical) using the Location Scorecard introduced in this lesson.

---

### Lesson 2: Reading a Street

**What it covers:** Foot traffic, daytime population, anchor tenants, and the difference between a street that looks busy and one that actually converts. You'll learn how to spend time on-site the right way and what free tools exist for pulling basic traffic data.

**Why it matters:** The best-looking storefront on a dead block is still a dead block. This lesson teaches you to separate activity from opportunity.

**Exercise:** Visit one candidate location during three different time windows (morning rush, midday, late afternoon) and log what you see. Bring your notes to Lesson 3.

---

### Lesson 3: Evaluating a Space

**What it covers:** What to look for when you walk through a potential space. Plumbing capacity, electrical load, grease trap requirements, HVAC for espresso equipment, and layout flow. You don't need to be a contractor, but you do need to know what questions to ask one.

**Why it matters:** Build-out surprises are the number one reason coffee shop budgets blow up. A $3,500/month space with a $120,000 build-out is a different deal than it looks.

**Exercise:** Download the Space Walkthrough Checklist and use it on your next site visit. Flag every item you can't verify yourself and bring that list to your first contractor conversation.

---

### Lesson 4: Understanding Your Lease

**What it covers:** The 10 lease terms that will most affect your business. Base rent vs. triple net (NNN), CAM charges, rent escalations, co-tenancy clauses, permitted use language, personal guarantees, and assignment rights. Plain-English explanations, not legal advice.

**Why it matters:** Most first-time tenants sign leases they don't fully understand. Knowing these terms before you sit down with a landlord changes the conversation.

**Exercise:** Pull any commercial lease (from your broker, a local listing, or a sample template) and find and label each of the 10 key terms.

---

### Lesson 5: Negotiating the Lease

**What it covers:** The five things almost every coffee shop owner can negotiate for, and most don't ask for. Tenant improvement (TI) allowance, free rent during build-out, a longer initial term with renewal options, a cap on CAM charge increases, and co-tenancy protections.

**Why it matters:** Landlords expect negotiation. The first offer is a starting point, not a final number. This lesson gives you specific language and realistic targets.

**Exercise:** Write your "ask list" for a hypothetical or real lease negotiation. Rank your items from must-have to nice-to-have so you know where you have room to give.

---

### Lesson 6: The Letter of Intent

**What it covers:** What an LOI is, why you should use one before the full lease, what goes in it, and what common mistakes to avoid. Includes a sample LOI structure you can adapt.

**Why it matters:** An LOI protects you. It lets you confirm the deal terms before your lawyer (and the landlord's lawyer) runs up the clock on a full lease.

**Exercise:** Draft a one-page LOI for your top candidate location using the template in this lesson. You're not sending it, you're practicing the structure.

---

### Lesson 7: Working with a Tenant Rep Broker

**What it covers:** What a tenant rep broker does, how they get paid (hint: usually by the landlord), when you should hire one, and how to find someone who actually knows the coffee and restaurant space in your market.

**Why it matters:** A good tenant rep can find spaces before they hit the market and run your negotiation for free. A bad one will push you toward whatever deal closes fastest. This lesson helps you tell the difference.

**Exercise:** Identify two tenant rep brokers in your target market and send them the same brief description of your concept, budget, and preferred zones. Compare how they respond.

# Lesson 1 (Full Draft): The Location Decision Framework

**Module 3, Lesson 1**
**Reading time:** 12 to 15 minutes

---

Before you walk through a single space, you need a way to evaluate it. Not just "does it feel right" or "is the rent low enough" but a real framework you can apply to any address in any market.

Here's the problem most first-time owners run into. They find a space they love. The street looks active, the landlord seems reasonable, the rent is within budget. So they sign. Six months after opening, they realize the foot traffic is all drive-by traffic from people going somewhere else. Or the lunch crowd is huge but the morning rush, when coffee shops make most of their money, is dead. Or the neighborhood is technically "up and coming" and has been for five years.

A framework doesn't stop you from making those mistakes. Doing the work does. But the framework tells you where to look.

**The three filters**

Every location decision comes down to three questions. Work through them in order, because the first one can eliminate a location before you spend time on the other two.

**Filter 1: Does the math work?**

Go back to your Module 2 numbers. You modeled your daily customer count and your average ticket. Those numbers assumed some level of foot traffic and visibility. Now you need to test whether this specific location can support those assumptions.

If you projected 120 customers a day, what would need to be true about this location for that to happen? Is there a daytime population within a quarter mile that could produce 120 coffee buyers? What are the comparable shops on the block or nearby doing? (You'll get better at estimating this in Lesson 2, but start asking the question now.)

If the math doesn't work at the rent being offered, no amount of charm or good coffee will fix it. Move on.

**Filter 2: Does it match your customer?**

In Module 1 you described your target customer in detail. You know their age range, what they value, how they use coffee as part of their day.

Now stand on the sidewalk outside this potential space and ask: is my customer actually here? Not "could they be here" or "this neighborhood is changing" but are they here today, in meaningful numbers, at the times I'd be open?

A specialty espresso bar built for remote workers needs neighborhoods where people actually work remotely, not neighborhoods where people commute through. A grab-and-go kiosk needs density and speed, not a quiet residential block where people linger.

If your target customer isn't already in the area, you're betting your business on changing behavior. That's a hard bet to win.

**Filter 3: Can the space actually work?**

This is where most people start, but it should always come third.

Once a location passes the first two filters, then you look at the physical space. Plumbing, electrical capacity, layout, ventilation, accessibility. We'll go deep on this in Lesson 3. For now, just know that a location can pass the first two filters and still fail on the physical reality of the space. Build-out costs and timeline can swing wildly based on what's already in the walls.

**How to use this in practice**

Print this framework out or copy it into your notes. Every time you look at a new address, run through the three filters before you schedule a showing. It will save you a lot of time looking at spaces that were never going to work.

One more thing: the best location you can afford is usually better than the cheapest location you can find. Location is the one thing you cannot change after you open. You can update your menu, retrain your staff, redo your branding. You cannot move a coffee shop.

List three locations you're considering or curious about. For each one, write two to three sentences on how it holds up against each filter. You don't need data yet, you're just training yourself to ask the questions in the right order.

Bring this list to Lesson 2, where you'll learn how to actually answer Filter 1 with real numbers.

# Space Walkthrough Checklist

**Module 3, Lesson 3 | Timberline Coffee Shop Consultant**

Bring this with you on every site visit. Walk the space before you sign anything.

---

## How to use this checklist

For each item: ask the question, compare what you see to "What good looks like," and flag it if it matches "Red flag." A red flag means get an answer in writing before you move forward.

---

## 1. Plumbing Capacity

| # | Question to ask | What good looks like | Red flag |
|---|----------------|---------------------|----------|
| 1.1 | How many drain lines come into this space, and where are they located? | At least two floor drains near the bar area; sink drains accessible without tearing into walls | Only one drain, or drains are across the room from where your bar will go |
| 1.2 | What is the water pressure at the tap? (Ask landlord or measure yourself with a gauge.) | 40-60 PSI at the tap consistently | Below 35 PSI or pressure that drops when a toilet flushes |
| 1.3 | Is there a grease interceptor on the line, and when was it last serviced? | Grease interceptor present and serviced within the past year | No interceptor, or landlord doesn't know what one is |
| 1.4 | What is the pipe diameter for the main water line? | 3/4-inch or larger for commercial use | 1/2-inch residential pipe. Undersized for a busy bar. |
| 1.5 | Can the space support a three-compartment sink plus a mop sink? | Enough floor drains and stub-outs to accommodate both without new trenching | Adding a sink would require cutting concrete |

---

## 2. Electrical Load

| # | Question to ask | What good looks like | Red flag |
|---|----------------|---------------------|----------|
| 2.1 | What is the total amperage of the electrical panel? | 200-amp panel minimum; 400-amp ideal for a full espresso bar | 100-amp panel shared with other tenants |
| 2.2 | Are there dedicated 20-amp circuits near the bar area? | At least 4-6 dedicated circuits in the service area | Only shared circuits, or all circuits already near capacity |
| 2.3 | What voltage is available at the panel: 120V only, or 240V? | Both 120V and 240V available | 120V only. Limits what equipment you can run. |
| 2.4 | Who is the utility provider and what is the monthly demand charge history? | Stable demand, no penalties on the bills | Demand charges that spike in summer or during peak hours |
| 2.5 | Has an electrician pulled permits in this space recently? Ask to see the permit history. | Clean permit history, no open violations | Open electrical violations or unpermitted work |

---

## 3. Grease Trap

| # | Question to ask | What good looks like | Red flag |
|---|----------------|---------------------|----------|
| 3.1 | Is there an existing grease trap or interceptor? Where is it located? | Interior trap under the sink area or exterior underground interceptor with a known size | No trap at all. You may need to install one before you open. |
| 3.2 | What is the capacity of the grease trap in gallons per minute (GPM)? | 20-50 GPM for a small to mid-size shop | Capacity unknown or undersized for a full kitchen line |
| 3.3 | What does the health department require for this address? | Health department has signed off on the current setup | Conflicting information between landlord and local health code |
| 3.4 | Who services the trap, and how often? | Regular service contract in place, pumped at least quarterly | Last pumped more than a year ago, or no service record exists |
| 3.5 | Will your coffee and food prep require a grease trap upgrade? | Current setup handles espresso, small pastry baking | Local code requires a larger trap if you add a fryer or full kitchen |

---

## 4. HVAC for Espresso

| # | Question to ask | What good looks like | Red flag |
|---|----------------|---------------------|----------|
| 4.1 | What is the HVAC system's total tonnage, and what square footage does it serve? | 1 ton per 300-350 square feet as a baseline for a coffee shop | Undersized unit that struggles on a 75-degree day |
| 4.2 | Is there ventilation above where your espresso machine and roaster will sit? | Existing hood or dedicated exhaust line that can be extended | Solid ceiling with no exhaust path. Adding ventilation means major work. |
| 4.3 | How old is the HVAC system? Ask for the last service record. | Unit 10 years old or newer, serviced within 12 months | Unit 15 years or older, no maintenance record |
| 4.4 | Can the system handle the heat load from an espresso machine running 8-10 hours a day? | Landlord or HVAC tech confirms it can; ideally you get this in writing | Vague answer, or "it worked fine for the last tenant" (who may not have run commercial equipment) |
| 4.5 | Is there makeup air for the hood if you add one? | Makeup air unit in place or space to add one | No makeup air and a hood is required. A significant cost to budget. |

---

## 5. Layout Flow

| # | Question to ask | What good looks like | Red flag |
|---|----------------|---------------------|----------|
| 5.1 | Where is the natural path customers will walk from the door to the register? | Clear sightline from entrance to counter; 36 inches minimum aisle width | Structural columns or walls in the customer path that cannot be moved |
| 5.2 | Can you position the bar so staff face customers and the espresso machine is within reach? | Bar area allows a barista to pull shots, steam milk, and hand off drinks without crossing the customer queue | Bar placement forces staff to turn their backs to customers or walk across a busy aisle |
| 5.3 | Is there a back-of-house area for receiving, storage, and prep separate from the customer area? | At least 150 square feet of back-of-house space | Everything in one open room. Nowhere to put deliveries or prep food away from customers. |
| 5.4 | Where do deliveries come in, and will they interfere with customers during business hours? | Loading area or back door that separates deliveries from the customer experience | Only a front door. Deliveries will happen in front of customers. |
| 5.5 | Is there enough space for the customer queue during your busiest period? Think: 15 people in line. | 10-12 feet of queue depth between the door and the counter | Fewer than 6 feet. The line blocks the door on a busy morning. |

---

## 6. Accessibility

| # | Question to ask | What good looks like | Red flag |
|---|----------------|---------------------|----------|
| 6.1 | Is the entrance ADA-compliant? Check the door width and any steps or lips at the threshold. | 32-inch clear doorway (36 inches preferred); zero step at the entrance or a functioning ramp | Steps with no ramp, or a door narrower than 32 inches |
| 6.2 | Is there an accessible path from the parking lot or street to the entrance? | Level or gently sloped path, no obstructions | Broken sidewalk, no curb cut, or a steep approach |
| 6.3 | Is at least one restroom accessible? Check door width, turning radius, and grab bars. | Restroom meets ADA specs or can be brought into compliance at reasonable cost | Restroom is too small to upgrade and there is no second option |
| 6.4 | Can the counter be designed with a lowered section for wheelchair users? | Space to include a 28-34 inch lowered counter section | Structural constraint that locks you into a single counter height throughout |
| 6.5 | Are there local accessibility upgrade requirements tied to your build-out scope? | Landlord and local building department confirm your planned scope doesn't trigger a full ADA upgrade | Landlord is vague, or your build-out budget may legally require you to bring the whole space into compliance |

## After the Visit

Write down anything that needs follow-up before you make an offer:

- Items flagged above: _______________________________________________
- Questions you couldn't get answered on-site: ___________________________
- Name and contact of the landlord's contact: ____________________________
- Date of visit: _______________________________________________________

Bring any red flags to a licensed contractor or your attorney before signing a lease.

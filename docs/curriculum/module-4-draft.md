# Module 4: Menu Design & Sourcing

**One-sentence description:** Build a menu that works financially and find a roasting partner you can actually rely on.

**Estimated completion time:** 2.5 to 3 hours across 4 to 6 sessions

---

## Who This Module Is For

You've done the conceptual work (Module 1), run the financial model (Module 2), and have a location strategy (Module 3). Now you need to figure out what you're actually selling.

Most owners treat the menu as the fun part. It is, but it's also where a lot of shops quietly fail. A menu that's too large slows your bar down. A menu that's underpriced doesn't cover your costs. A roasting partner who can't keep up with your volume creates chaos six months in. This module helps you get all three right before you open.

---

## Learning Objectives

By the end of this module, you will be able to:

- Design a menu that fits your shop model, your staff, and your projected volume
- Calculate COGS for your top menu items and price them to hit your margin targets
- Evaluate roasting partners on the things that actually matter at scale
- Build a supplier map that covers your core categories with a backup for each
- Produce a Menu Blueprint document you can share with investors, partners, and your build-out contractor

---

## Module 4 Lessons

### Lesson 1: Menu Strategy

**What it covers:** The mental model behind a menu that works. How many items is too many? What categories belong on a specialty coffee bar? What drives your average ticket and why that number matters more than most owners think.

**Why it matters:** A menu is a production system. Every item you add is a training requirement, an inventory line, and a variable in your speed of service equation. This lesson helps you think like a systems designer, not a customer.

**Exercise:** Write a one-sentence description of your shop model, then list every category you're considering for your menu. For each one, write whether it's core to your model or nice to have.

---

### Lesson 2: COGS and Pricing

**What it covers:** What cost of goods sold means for a coffee bar, target COGS percentages by category, and how to work backward from your margin target to your price. Includes a simple recipe costing method you can run in a spreadsheet.

**Why it matters:** Most first-time owners price based on what they see at other shops. That's fine if the other shop is profitable, which you have no way of knowing. Pricing from your own cost structure is the only way to know your margins before you open.

**Exercise:** Pick your three most likely espresso drinks. Price out every ingredient in one serving and calculate your COGS at the price you had in mind. Compare to the 18-28% target.

---

### Lesson 3: Finding the Right Roaster

**What it covers:** What to look for in a roasting partner beyond the coffee itself. Lead times, minimum orders, private label options, wholesale pricing structure, and what good service actually looks like when something goes wrong.

**Why it matters:** Sample bags taste great. The test is month six, when you're out of Guatemala and your roaster takes five days to ship. This lesson helps you evaluate a roasting relationship the way a business owner would.

**Exercise:** Identify two roasters you're interested in. Send each one the same email: your projected weekly volume, whether you want private label or branded bags, and a specific question about their fulfillment lead time. Compare how they respond.

---

### Lesson 4: Building Your Supplier Map

**What it covers:** Beyond coffee, what you need and from whom. Dairy and non-dairy milk (local vs. distributor), cups and lids, syrups and sauces, food items if you're carrying them, and equipment consumables. Primary supplier, backup supplier, and estimated monthly spend for each category.

**Why it matters:** Single-source risk is real. If your milk distributor has a delivery problem the week before your soft open, you need to know who you're calling next. This lesson makes sure you have an answer before you need one.

**Exercise:** Fill out the Supplier Map template for at least five categories. For each, write your primary source and identify at least one backup.

---

### Lesson 5: Your Menu Blueprint

**What it covers:** How to pull everything from this module into one document. The Menu Blueprint is your canonical reference for menu scope, pricing, sourcing decisions, and supplier relationships. It's the document you hand to investors, your build-out contractor (so they know what equipment to plan for), and your first hire.

**Why it matters:** A decision you made in your head on a Tuesday will be forgotten by the time your build-out is done. The Menu Blueprint captures your reasoning while it's fresh.

**Exercise:** Complete your Menu Blueprint using the template in this lesson. Everything else in this module feeds into it.

# Lesson 1 (Full Draft): Menu Strategy

**Module 4, Lesson 1**
**Reading time:** 12 to 14 minutes

---

Before you write a single item on your menu, you need a mental model for what a menu is supposed to do.

Here's the trap most first-time owners fall into. They think about the menu as a list of things they love or things they've seen at shops they admire. They add a lavender latte because it photographs well. They add avocado toast because the coffee shop down the street has it. They add six different signature drinks because they want to stand out. Six months into service, their barista is guessing at ratios, their prep list is 45 minutes long before they open, and their average ticket is three dollars lower than their model assumed.

A menu is not a list of things you'd like to serve. It's a production system. Every item you add is a training requirement, an inventory line, a prep step, and a variable in your speed of service equation. A well-designed menu makes the bar easier to run, not harder.

**Start with your model**

Go back to your Module 1 concept. You described the kind of shop you're building: the pace, the customer, the vibe, what people come in for. Your menu should serve that concept, not the other way around.

A grab-and-go kiosk in a downtown office tower needs a tight menu. Five to seven espresso drinks, a couple of batch brew options, one or two food items that can be handed across a counter in under a minute. Every additional item is a transaction that might take forty-five seconds longer, which adds up fast when you're pulling 150 covers before noon.

A neighborhood sit-down cafe has more room. The pace is slower, the customer is less rushed, and menu variety can drive ticket average and repeat visits. But "more room" doesn't mean unconstrained. Twenty drinks and a full kitchen is still a staffing problem if you're opening with two people.

The question isn't how many items you want. It's how many items your shop model can actually support.

**The specialty coffee menu**

If you're opening a specialty coffee bar, your espresso program is the center of everything. Start there.

A standard specialty espresso bar has four to six espresso-based drinks at its core: a cappuccino, a latte, a cortado or macchiato, and a flat white covers most customers most of the time. You'll add your own signature drink or two, and that's usually enough variety without overwhelming your bar.

Then decide: are you offering batch brew? Cold brew? Non-coffee alternatives for the person who walks in with someone who drinks coffee but doesn't themselves? These decisions depend on your customer and your volume projections.

One rule that holds up consistently: don't add a category until you can run it well. A mediocre matcha latte is worse for your brand than no matcha latte.

**What drives your average ticket**

Most owners think about average ticket as a result of what customers order. That's true, but it's also something you can influence through menu design.

The biggest levers are food and size options.

If you're carrying food, even a small pastry program from a local bakery, you can add three to five dollars to a percentage of your transactions without putting any load on your bar. The customer who buys a coffee and a croissant has a higher ticket than the one who buys coffee alone. This is obvious in theory, but easy to skip when you're focused on the coffee program.

Size options are a subtler version of the same idea. Offering a twelve-ounce and a sixteen-ounce latte is one way to let customers self-select into a higher ticket. But don't add sizes just to have them. More size options mean more variables in recipe consistency and more decision time at the register.

**The exercise**

Before you build out your full menu, do this first.

Write a one-sentence description of your shop model. Something like: "A fast-casual espresso bar targeting weekday morning commuters in a 400-square-foot footprint with two staff" or "A 1,200-square-foot neighborhood cafe with table service and a focus on remote workers who stay for two-plus hours."

Then list every category you're considering: espresso drinks, batch brew, cold brew, tea, non-coffee drinks, pastries, sandwiches, other food. For each one, write one word: core or optional.

Core means the category is essential to your concept and your customer will expect it. Optional means it's something you're considering but could cut if your model required it.

This exercise usually surfaces one or two categories that are optional in reality but felt essential before you wrote them down. That's the point. Every optional category you remove makes your bar easier to train, faster to run, and more consistent. Your customers will notice the quality of fewer things done well before they notice the absence of things you didn't offer.

Bring this list to Lesson 2, where you'll price out your core items and find out whether the menu you're designing actually hits your margin targets.

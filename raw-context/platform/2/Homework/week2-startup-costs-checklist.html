What Does It Actually
Cost to Open?

This checklist covers every major cost category involved in opening a coffee shop. Use it to make sure nothing gets left out of your startup budget — and to get a realistic feel for the range before you commit to anything.

✅ Week 2 — Startup Costs Checklist

Startup costs are the one-time costs to get your shop from idea to open. They're separate from your ongoing monthly costs — rent, wages, ingredients — which belong in your financial model. This checklist organises startup costs by category so you can work through them one at a time and build up an honest estimate.

How to use this: Go through each category. Mark the items that apply to your shop type. Add your best estimate in the margin or in your own notes. At the end, total it up — then add your working capital reserve on top (explained at the bottom of this sheet).

Lean build
$50K – $90K
Small footprint, second-hand equipment, simple fit-out
Mid-range build
$90K – $200K
New equipment, custom fit-out, standard lease
Full build
$200K – $400K+
Drive-through, large space, or full-spec specialty build

A word of caution: These ranges are starting points, not promises. Your actual number depends heavily on your city, your space, and your choices. The most important thing is to build your own estimate from the line items below — don't start with a total and work backwards.

☕ Espresso & Brewing Equipment

Often the biggest single cost category
Item Notes Rough Range
Espresso machine Core A quality commercial machine is one of the most important investments. New mid-range machines start around $8K. High-spec specialty machines run $20K+. Used equipment can reduce this significantly — but factor in service history. $8K – $25K+
Grinder(s) Core You need at least one dedicated espresso grinder. Busy shops use one per group head to avoid bottlenecks. Specialty shops often add a separate grinder for filter. Budget for multiple if your volume will be high. $1.5K – $4K each
Water filtration system Core Not optional. Hard water destroys equipment and ruins coffee quality. Your machine supplier should be able to advise on what's right for your local water. Installation is usually separate. $500 – $2K
Batch brew / filter coffee equipment If relevant If you plan to serve filter or drip coffee. Ranges from a simple commercial brewer to more elaborate pour-over setups. Consider how you'll serve it — pour-over adds labour, batch brew is faster. $500 – $3K
Coffee tamper, accessories, and tools Tampers, milk pitchers, shot glasses, cleaning brushes, distribution tools. Easy to underestimate — budget at least $500 for a full bar setup. $300 – $800

🧊 Refrigeration & Food Equipment

Scales up significantly if you're adding food
Item Notes Rough Range
Under-counter refrigeration Core Essential for milk storage close to the bar. Most bars need at least one undercounter fridge within reach of the barista. $800 – $2.5K
Display fridge / pastry case If relevant If you're selling grab-and-go food, drinks, or displayed pastries. Front-facing display fridges are more expensive than back-of-house storage. $1.5K – $5K
Blender(s) If relevant Required if you're selling blended drinks, smoothies, or any iced blended menu items. Commercial blenders designed for high-volume use are a must — consumer-grade ones won't hold up. $600 – $1.5K each
Food prep equipment If relevant For cafés with a food offering. Could include a sandwich press, toaster oven, hot hold unit, or a small kitchen setup depending on the scope of your food menu. This category can grow quickly. $1K – $15K+
Ice machine If relevant Needed if you're serving iced drinks at any volume. Countertop models work for lower volume; under-counter or freestanding machines for higher demand. Factor in water line installation. $800 – $3K

💳 Point of Sale & Technology

Often overlooked in first estimates
Item Notes Rough Range
POS system (hardware + setup) Core Includes the tablet or terminal, cash drawer, receipt printer, and card reader. Systems like Square, Toast, or Lightspeed have different pricing structures — some have lower upfront hardware costs but higher monthly fees. Compare the total cost over 12 months, not just the setup cost. $800 – $3K setup + monthly fees
Internet & networking setup Core Reliable internet is essential for your POS and payments. If you're offering Wi-Fi to customers, you may need a separate network for guests and a stronger router. Installation by a professional is worth it to avoid ongoing issues. $300 – $1.5K
Music licensing Core Playing music in a commercial space requires a license. Services like SOCAN (Canada), APRA (Australia), or PRS (UK) issue these. Cost varies by space size. Budget this as a recurring annual cost rather than a one-time expense. $250 – $800/year
Security system / cameras If relevant Basic security cameras and alarm systems are worth considering, especially if your space is in a high-traffic area or if there's overnight stock stored on site. $500 – $2K

🏗️ Fit-Out, Furniture & Signage

Highly variable — depends on condition of the space and your design choices
Item Notes Rough Range
Bar build and counter Core Your bar is the heart of the shop and can be one of the most significant fit-out costs. A simple, clean bar can be built reasonably. A custom-designed specialty bar will cost more. Get at least three quotes from local contractors before deciding. $10K – $40K+
Flooring, walls, and painting Many spaces need at least some cosmetic work before opening. Factor in floor treatment, painting, and any wall features that are part of your concept. Your landlord may contribute to some of this — it's worth asking. $3K – $20K+
Seating and tables Sit-down shops Budget per seat — a simple wood stool and small table is very different from upholstered seating and custom furniture. Second-hand commercial furniture is a smart option for early-stage shops. $200 – $600 per seat
Lighting Lighting has a massive impact on atmosphere and costs less than most people think to upgrade. Budget for both functional lighting over the bar and the ambient lighting that shapes how the space feels. $2K – $10K
Exterior and interior signage Core Includes your front sign, window graphics or lettering, and any internal signage (menu boards, directional signs). Depends heavily on your lease — some buildings have signage restrictions. $1.5K – $8K
Menu boards Chalkboard, printed panels, or digital screens — each has different cost and flexibility. Digital screens make updating easy but cost more upfront. Budget for initial design regardless of format. $500 – $3K

📋 Permits, Licensing & Legal

Varies significantly by location — check your city or region early
Item Notes Rough Range
Business registration and setup Core Registering your business structure (sole trader, partnership, corporation). Costs vary by region and structure. If you're setting up a corporation or LLC, you may want a lawyer or accountant to help with this. $200 – $2K
Food handler / food safety certification Core Most jurisdictions require at least one person in the business to hold a food safety certificate. Usually a one-day course. Budget for yourself and potentially a key member of staff. $100 – $400 per person
Health department inspection and permit Core Required before you can open. The inspection checks your space against local food safety standards. Build extra time into your opening timeline to allow for any required changes before you pass inspection. $200 – $1K
Building permit (if doing construction) If relevant Any significant structural changes — plumbing, electrical, or construction — typically require a permit. Your contractor should be able to advise on this, but make sure it's not overlooked in the timeline. $500 – $3K+
Legal review of lease Core Do not sign a commercial lease without a lawyer reviewing it. This is one of the highest-impact investments you'll make. A lease is typically 3–5 years and binds you to significant ongoing costs. The cost of a review is small compared to the cost of a bad lease. $500 – $2K
Liquor licence If relevant Only applies if you're planning to sell alcohol (wine, beer, spirits). Liquor licences are complex, take time to process, and vary significantly in cost by region. If this is part of your plan, start the process early. $500 – $5K+

📦 Opening Inventory & Supplies

What you need to have on hand on day one
Item Notes Rough Range
Opening coffee stock Core Your initial order of beans, including your espresso blend and any single-origins or filter offerings. Talk to your roaster about the right opening quantity — you want enough to not run out but not so much that it goes stale. $500 – $1.5K
Cups, lids, and packaging Core Takeaway cups in multiple sizes, lids, sleeves, stirrers, napkins, and bags. If you're using custom-printed cups, order lead times can be 4–6 weeks. Minimum order quantities are often higher than expected. $1K – $3K
Opening food stock If relevant For shops with a food offer — pastries, baked goods, grab-and-go items. If you're sourcing from a local bakery, your opening order is typically smaller. If you're making in-house, ingredient stock will be higher. $500 – $2K
Cleaning supplies and equipment Core Commercial cleaning products, mop and bucket, cloths, sanitiser dispensers, grease trap supplies, and anything needed for daily cleaning of your equipment. Don't underestimate this category — commercial cleaning costs more than household. $400 – $1K
Staff uniforms or branded apparel If relevant Aprons, branded t-shirts, or any uniform components. Not all shops use uniforms — but if it's part of your concept, budget for at least 2–3 sets per staff member at opening. $20 – $60 per item

🔑 Lease Deposits & Pre-Opening Fees

Money you need before you open the door
Item Notes Rough Range
Lease security deposit Core Usually 2–4 months of rent paid upfront as a deposit. This is refundable at the end of the lease if you leave the space in good condition, but it's money tied up from day one. Negotiate this — landlords will sometimes reduce it. 2–4 months rent
First and last month's rent Core Many commercial leases require first and last month paid at signing, on top of the security deposit. This is a significant upfront cash requirement — make sure it's built into your startup number. 2 months rent
Fit-out or leasehold improvement deposit Some landlords charge a separate deposit for the right to make changes to the space. Others contribute to fit-out costs (called a "tenant improvement allowance") if you negotiate it into the lease. Worth asking about. $0 – $10K+

💰 Working Capital — the number most people forget

Working capital is the cash reserve you need to keep the business running while it's building up to a steady income. In the first few months, you'll almost certainly be spending more than you're bringing in — that gap needs to be funded from somewhere.

A safe rule of thumb: Budget for at least 3–4 months of your full operating costs (rent + wages + ingredients + everything else) as a working capital reserve, on top of your startup costs. For most independent coffee shops, this means having an additional $25,000 to $75,000 in reserve before you open.

This is not money you're spending — it's money you need to have available. Some of it you'll use. Some of it you won't. But if you don't have it and you hit a slow patch, your options narrow very quickly.

Don't forget the 15–20% buffer. Whatever total you arrive at from this checklist, add 15–20% on top. Construction always uncovers surprises. Equipment quotes don't always include installation. Lead times create unexpected costs. The buffer is not a luxury — it's a planning tool.

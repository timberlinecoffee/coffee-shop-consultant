Week 5 — Bar Layout & Equipment  ·  Homework Brief

Most bar layout mistakes look fine on paper. The espresso machine is there, the grinder is next to it, the fridge is nearby. It all makes sense until a barista tries to make six drinks at once — and realises they have to turn around to reach the milk, step sideways to access the cups, and lean across someone else to get to the filter station.

Your bar layout is not a design decision. It's an operational decision. Every unnecessary step, every awkward reach, every time a person has to cross the path of another person — that's time, and time is what limits how many drinks you can serve in a morning rush. This week you map it all out before a single thing gets built.

Your Three Tasks This Week
Work through all three before the live call. Task 1 is the foundation — Tasks 2 and 3 build directly from it.
1
Layout Task

Sketch your bar layout and identify your service zones

Draw a top-down floor plan of your bar — it doesn't need to be to scale, but it does need to be honest about the actual dimensions of your space. Place your major equipment in the sketch: espresso machine, grinder(s), underbench fridge, brew bar or filter station, blender if applicable, till and payment point, cup storage, and customer handoff point.

Then identify your three service zones and label them clearly:

Order zone — where the customer places their order and pays.
Production zone — where the drink is made. This should be your largest and most carefully thought-out zone.
Handoff zone — where the finished drink is collected by the customer. Ideally separate from the order point so the queue and the pickup don't collide.

If you don't have a space yet: Design for your target shop type instead. Use the dimensions typical for your model — a grab-and-go bar might be 3m of counter, a neighbourhood café might have a full back-bar setup. Being specific about the type of space you're designing for will make the live call more useful for you.
2
Equipment Task

Build your equipment list and get real numbers

List every piece of equipment you'll need to open. For each item, note whether you're buying new, buying used, or leasing — and find a real price for each option. Getting actual quotes at this stage, even rough ones, is the point. Equipment is usually one of the biggest capital costs in a coffee shop opening, and most people underestimate it significantly when they're planning.

Your list should include at minimum: espresso machine, grinder (likely two — one for espresso blend, one for single origin or decaf), underbench fridge, commercial coffee grinder knock box and tamping setup, water filtration system, batch brew or filter setup if applicable, POS system, and any food equipment if you're offering food.

On new vs. used: A used commercial espresso machine from a reputable dealer can save you thousands and still perform reliably — but factor in a service check before purchase. A used machine with unknown history is a risk. If you're leasing, make sure you understand the total cost over the term, not just the monthly payment.
3
Workflow Task

Trace a drink through your bar and find the bottlenecks

Pick your highest-volume drink — almost certainly a dairy flat white or latte — and trace every physical step required to make it from the moment the order is placed to the moment it's handed to the customer. Use your bar sketch from Task 1 and draw the path with arrows.

Then ask three questions: Where does the barista have to move the most? Where do two people's paths cross during a rush? Where would the process slow down if there was a queue of eight people waiting?

Every bottleneck you find on paper is a bottleneck you can fix before you build. Every one you miss on paper will cost you real money — and real stress — once you open.

The two-barista test: Imagine two people working your bar simultaneously during a busy period. Can they move without getting in each other's way? Can one pull shots while the other steams milk without bumping into each other? If the answer is no, your layout needs a rethink before anything else.

The most common layout mistake: Designing the bar to look good in a photo rather than work well in a rush. Customers face the front. Staff face the back. The bar you design needs to be optimised for the person making drinks — not the person photographing the shop for Instagram. A beautiful bar that creates a slow service line will hurt your reviews, your revenue, and your staff far more than a plain bar that runs like clockwork.

📋

What to bring to the live call

Bring your bar sketch and your equipment list with prices. They don't need to be finished — rough is fine. The live call will walk through a few layouts together, looking specifically at service flow and the placement of equipment relative to the workflow you traced in Task 3. We'll also compare equipment costs across the group, because the range of what people find can be significant and that information is valuable to everyone.

Outside resources worth looking at
You don't need to read everything — but these are worth having in your back pocket this week.
🏪

Stand at a café counter and watch the bar during a rush

Ask the owner or manager if you can observe for 20 minutes on a busy morning. Watch how many times a barista has to change direction, where they slow down, and how two people navigate sharing the space. You'll learn more from 20 minutes of observation than from any diagram.

🔧

Talk to a coffee equipment supplier or dealer

Equipment suppliers talk to hundreds of café owners every year. They've seen what works and what doesn't. A good dealer will tell you honestly whether your proposed setup is well-suited to your volume — and they'll often have strong opinions about layout. That perspective is worth getting early.

📐

Look at café fit-out companies and their past projects

Many café fit-out and joinery companies post case studies of completed projects — including floor plans and before-and-after photos. Browsing these gives you a sense of what well-designed bars look like in the real world and what questions to ask your builder or fit-out team.

💬

Ask a working barista what they wish was different about their bar

This is probably the most valuable research you can do this week. A barista who has worked behind a badly laid-out bar will tell you exactly what the problems were. That knowledge is hard-won and freely shared — most people who've worked in bad setups are happy to talk about it.

The Three Service Zones

Every coffee bar — regardless of size or shop type — has three distinct zones. Designing each one deliberately, and keeping them from colliding with each other, is the single most important layout decision you'll make.

Zone 1
Order Zone
Where the customer places their order and pays

This is where the customer interacts directly with staff. It needs to feel welcoming and unhurried — even when the queue is long. The menu should be clearly visible from this point before the customer reaches the counter.

Keep it simple: till, payment terminal, a spot to stand. Don't clutter this area with equipment, cups, or coffee kit. It's the customer's zone, not the barista's.

If your order zone and handoff zone are in the same place, your queue will always block the pickup — that slows down service and frustrates customers who've already paid.
Zone 2
Production Zone
Where every drink gets made

This is your engine room. It should be your largest zone, your most ergonomically considered space, and the one you spend the most time thinking about. Everything a barista needs to make every drink on your menu must be within a short reach — without crossing paths with another person.

The golden triangle: espresso machine, grinder, and milk fridge should form a tight triangle that a barista can work within without turning around or taking more than one step in any direction.

Every step a barista takes during a rush is time lost. If making your most common drink requires four body movements, redesign until it requires two.
Zone 3
Handoff Zone
Where the finished drink is collected

Separate from the order zone wherever possible. Customers collecting their drinks should not be standing in the same physical space as customers placing their orders — that creates a pinch point that slows everything down.

Name boards, buzzers, or a simple call-out all work fine. The key is that the person collecting does not need to stand in the queue to do it.

For grab-and-go bars, even a 40cm separation between order and handoff makes a meaningful difference to queue flow during peak times.
Design Best Practices

These principles apply before you think about equipment. They're about how people — both customers and baristas — move through your space. Get the flow right first, then figure out where the machines go.

→
Flow Principle 1
Design for one-directional flow — for everyone
Customer flow

Customers should move through your space in one direction — enter, browse or queue, order, wait or move aside, collect, leave. If customers are doubling back on themselves to collect a drink, or cutting across people still waiting to order, your layout is working against you.

Think of it like a river with a clear current. You want every person to know instinctively which way to go next without having to read a sign or ask someone. That clarity comes from layout, not signage.

Barista flow

The same principle applies behind the bar. A barista's movement through a drink — from grind to tamp to extract to steam to pour to serve — should flow in one direction along the bar, not zigzag back and forth. If making a flat white requires a barista to move left, then right, then left again, the bar is fighting them.

Watch a well-designed bar during a rush and you'll see baristas moving with a rhythm. Watch a poorly designed one and you'll see them dancing around each other, reaching past each other, and losing seconds on every drink.

The test: Draw arrows on your bar sketch showing where the customer walks and where the barista moves to make your most common drink. If either set of arrows doubles back on itself, that's where your flow breaks down.
👁
Flow Principle 2
Design the customer journey before you order anything

Walk your space — or your sketched space — as if you are a customer entering for the first time. Ask yourself at each step: What do I see? What am I drawn toward? Where do I go next? When do I feel comfortable?

The customer journey in a coffee shop typically has five moments. Each one is a design opportunity — or a friction point waiting to happen.

1
Enter

Can they immediately see where to go? Is there a clear visual pull toward the ordering point? Or do they stop, look around, and feel uncertain? Uncertain customers slow down the flow before they've even joined the queue.

2
Browse & queue

Can they read the menu from where they're standing before they reach the till? This is where retail products and the pastry display belong — along the customer's path before they reach the Point of Sale. Not after it. Not beside it. Before it. A customer who passes your retail display and pastry case on the way to the till has already considered adding something. A customer who only sees it after they've paid has already closed their wallet mentally.

3
Order & pay

Is the ordering experience calm for the staff member taking the order? If the person on the till is also making drinks, or reaching across the bar, or being interrupted by workflow behind them, the customer feels that tension. The order zone should feel like a clear, dedicated interaction — not a side task.

4
Wait

Where does the customer go after ordering? If there's nowhere natural to wait — or if the waiting area puts them in the path of people still ordering — you'll get a pinch point. Give people somewhere obvious to stand or sit that keeps them out of the queue and within earshot of the handoff call.

5
Collect & leave (or stay)

Is the handoff point visible and clearly theirs to approach? Does collecting their drink require walking back through the queue? A smooth handoff is the last impression of the service experience — it should feel frictionless, not like an obstacle course.

🥐
Revenue Principle
Retail and pastry display: before the till, not beside it

This is one of the highest-leverage layout decisions you can make for average transaction value — and most first-time operators get it wrong by accident. The pastry display and any retail products (coffee bags, merchandise, gift cards) should sit along the customer's path before they reach the POS. Here is why this matters:

Before the POS ✓

Customer walks in, sees the pastry display, pauses to consider. By the time they reach the till, they've already half-decided to add a croissant. The barista asks "anything with your coffee?" and the answer is yes more often. The decision was made in the walk, not at the counter.

Beside or after the POS ✗

Customer reaches the till, gives their order, pays. The transaction is complete in their mind. Even if there's a beautiful pastry display right beside them, they've mentally closed the purchase. Add-on rates drop significantly once someone has already paid.

Practical note: If your layout genuinely cannot place the display before the POS, the next best option is having it visible and reachable from the ordering position — so the barista can point to it naturally. A display that is behind the customer, or in a corner they passed before they decided to queue, does very little work.
⚡
Flow Principle 3
Identify and remove friction points — for both people

A friction point is anything that causes either the customer or the barista to slow down, hesitate, or work harder than they should. Most friction points are invisible until you're in the middle of a morning rush — which is exactly why you need to find them in the planning stage.

Customer friction points to eliminate
Not knowing where to queue. If the queue direction isn't obvious, the first few customers will work it out — but every new customer arriving during a rush has to figure it out again. A clear, physical queue line (even just counter design) removes this entirely.
Can't read the menu until they reach the counter. They hold up the queue while they decide. The solution is a menu board visible from at least 3 metres back, not at the point of ordering.
Nowhere natural to wait after ordering. They either hover at the till (blocking the next order) or drift too far from the handoff point and miss their name being called. A designated waiting area — even just a visual cue — solves this.
Having to walk back through the queue to collect. If the handoff point is at the start of the queue, collected customers are walking against the flow of people still arriving. Separate these wherever possible.
Barista friction points to eliminate
Any reach that requires crossing the body. Reaching across yourself to access equipment or ingredients is a micro-inefficiency that adds up. Every regular movement should be a natural extension of where the barista already is.
Shared access points between two baristas. If both baristas need the same piece of equipment or the same part of the bench at the same time during peak service, one of them is always waiting. Design around this or duplicate the equipment.
No clear home for everything. Anything without a defined place will drift — and staff will spend time looking for things mid-service. Every piece of equipment, every tool, every supply should have one place it lives and returns to.
Cleaning access that conflicts with service flow. If the bin is in the way of the workflow, it won't be emptied on time. If the cleaning sink requires crossing the bar, it won't be used mid-service. Design cleaning into the workflow, not as an afterthought.
Equipment Placement Principles

These aren't design preferences — they're operational rules that experienced baristas and fit-out specialists have learned the hard way.

PrincipleWhat it means in practice
Put the espresso machine where the barista faces the customerStandard for most cafés The barista should be able to look up and make eye contact with the customer while pulling shots. It creates connection, speeds up communication about the order, and lets them call out names without turning around.
Grinder goes immediately to the left or right of the machineNever across the bar Every time a barista has to reach across themselves — or worse, step away — to get ground coffee, you're adding 5–10 seconds to every drink. Over 100 drinks a day, that's real time. Keep the grinder within one arm's reach of the group head.
Milk fridge at knee height, directly below the steam wandOr as close as possible Reaching behind for milk is one of the most common layout errors. Underbench fridges directly below the steam wand mean a barista can grab milk, steam it, and return it without moving their feet. This one change can dramatically speed up service.
Cups live between the grinder and the handoff pointNot at the far end of the bar If a barista has to walk three steps to get a cup at the start of every drink, cup placement is your bottleneck. Cups should be stacked within the production zone, within reach of where the drink starts being built.
Keep the filter and espresso stations physically separateFor bars serving both If you're serving both espresso drinks and filter coffee, treat them as two distinct workstations. A barista working batch brew should not be crossing the espresso bar to do so. This matters most when two people are working simultaneously.
Plan 900mm of clear working space per baristaPer person, not shared Each barista working the bar needs approximately 900mm of counter space to work in without getting in someone else's way. A bar with 1800mm of working counter can comfortably accommodate two people. Less than that and they'll be bumping into each other during the rush.
Waste and knock box go where the barista naturally standsNot somewhere convenient for cleaning The knock box should be directly at the barista's working position — not across the bar where it's easier to clean. If a barista has to lean or step to knock spent grounds, that friction accumulates across hundreds of drinks a day.
Equipment You Need to Open

This is a guide to what's essential and what's optional depending on your shop type and menu. Use the Equipment Checklist to record your actual quotes alongside these categories.

Espresso Bar — Core Equipment

☕
Essential

Commercial espresso machine

2-group minimum for most cafés. Consider a 3-group if you're expecting high volume. Brands like La Marzocco, Synesso, Victoria Arduino, and Sanremo are industry standards. New: $12,000–$25,000+. Used: $4,000–$10,000.

⚙️
Essential

Espresso grinder (×2 recommended)

One for your house blend, one for single origin or decaf. Brands: Mahlkönig, Mythos, Eureka. A single grinder forces staff to re-dial between coffees — not practical during a rush. New: $2,500–$5,000 each.

🧊
Essential

Underbench milk fridge

Commercial underbench refrigeration directly below or adjacent to the steam wand. Must hold temperature below 4°C. Factor in capacity for your peak milk volume — running out mid-service is a real problem.

💧
Essential

Water filtration system

Protects your espresso machine from scale and mineral build-up, and significantly affects flavour. Often overlooked and always regretted when skipped. Factor in ongoing filter cartridge costs.

🔧
Essential

Tamper, distribution tool, knock box

Quality tamping and distribution tools pay for themselves in shot consistency. Knock box should be built-in to the bench or at minimum mounted — freestanding knock boxes shift and slow the workflow.

🌡️
Essential

Steam wand thermometers / milk pitchers

Multiple pitchers in different sizes (300ml, 600ml, 1L) for different drink volumes. Thermometers for training and consistency — particularly important if you're training staff who don't yet have reliable temperature feel.

Filter & Cold — Based on Your Menu

☕
Essential if serving filter

Commercial batch brewer

Fetco, Marco, or similar commercial batch brewers. Must hold temperature without continuing to cook the coffee. Batch brew is your highest-margin drink — don't underinvest here. New: $2,500–$5,000.

⚖️
Optional / specialty

Pour-over station and scales

If you're offering manual pour-over. Requires a grinder capable of filter grind sizes (usually a separate grinder from espresso), quality scales, and a gooseneck kettle. Adds time per drink — factor into workflow.

🧊
Optional / recommended

Cold brew equipment

If cold brew is on your menu, you'll need a large cold brew brewing vessel (stainless, commercial grade) and refrigerated storage. Cold brew takes 12–18 hours to brew — plan storage accordingly.

🥤
Optional

Commercial blender

Only if you're serving blended drinks (frappes, smoothies). Vitamix or Blendtec commercial models. Factor in noise — a blender running during quiet mornings is noticeable. Needs its own designated counter space.

Operations & Technology

💳
Essential

Point of sale (POS) system

Square, Lightspeed, Kounta, or similar. Needs to handle split payments, run basic sales reports, and ideally integrate with your accounting software. Factor in monthly subscription costs, not just hardware.

🖨️
Essential

Receipt and order printers

Thermal receipt printer for the till, and a kitchen/bar printer if your workflow needs printed drink tickets. Wireless printers reduce cable runs but require a reliable Wi-Fi connection.

📦
Essential

Commercial dishwasher

A commercial underbench glasswasher is the minimum for a sit-down café. For takeaway-heavy operations, assess your cup and vessel volume — you may be able to manage with a smaller unit initially.

🔌
Essential

Power and electrical fit-out

Commercial espresso machines require 3-phase power in most markets. Your electrician needs to be involved before you finalise bar placement — not after. Moving a machine because power can't reach it is expensive.

New, Used, or Leased?

There's no universally right answer — but there are right answers for your situation. Here's how to think through each option honestly.

Option 1

Buy New

Full warranty, known history, current technology. You own the asset outright and have no ongoing payments.

✓ Warranty and manufacturer support. No surprises on reliability. May qualify for tax depreciation.

✗ Highest upfront cost. Ties up capital you might need elsewhere in the first year.

Best for: Operators with solid opening capital who want zero maintenance risk in year one.

Option 2

Buy Used

Significant savings on commercial machines that hold their value well. A well-maintained used La Marzocco can outlast a poorly maintained new machine.

✓ Major cost saving. Quality machines depreciate — buying 3 years old means someone else absorbed that hit.

✗ No warranty. Unknown service history. Budget for a full service check before purchase — always.

Best for: Budget-conscious operators who can source machines through reputable dealers with service records.

Option 3

Lease

Lower upfront cost. Often includes a service agreement. Some roasters offer equipment as part of their wholesale deal — worth asking.

✓ Preserves opening capital. Service often included. Roaster-supplied equipment can come with training.

✗ Total cost over term is usually more than buying outright. Read the terms on what happens if you exit early.

Best for: Operators with limited opening capital, or those whose roaster offers a tied equipment arrangement.

The Most Common Layout Mistakes

These are the ones that show up most often — and the ones that are most expensive to fix after you've already built.

Ordering and handoff in the same spot

The queue for ordering clogs the area where completed drinks sit. People reaching past queueing customers to collect their drinks slows everything down and creates friction.

Fix: Physically separate the handoff point from the till by at least 60–80cm.

Grinder too far from the machine

Even 30cm extra reach per shot adds up to minutes per hour. Over 80 drinks a day, it's genuinely significant — and it's usually caused by aesthetic decisions, not operational ones.

Fix: Grinder goes immediately beside the machine. Non-negotiable.

Milk fridge behind the barista

Reaching behind yourself to retrieve milk means turning your back to the machine mid-drink, risking over-extraction while you're turned away. Underbench storage solves this completely.

Fix: Underbench fridge directly below or beside the steam wand position.

Not testing the layout with two people

Most bar layouts are designed by one person imagining one person working. The morning rush usually involves two. If two people can't move freely without bumping into each other, the layout will create daily frustration.

Fix: Walk through your bar sketch with a second person before anything is built.

Designing for how it looks in photos

Open shelving looks great on Instagram. Cold brew towers look impressive. Exposed copper fixtures look beautiful. None of them help your team make drinks faster. Operational function should win every time there's a conflict with aesthetics.

Fix: If an aesthetic choice makes the workflow worse, cut it.

No plan for a menu board that's readable

Customers who can't read the menu before they reach the till cause queue slowdowns. A menu board that requires the customer to be standing at the counter to read it means every customer takes longer to order.

Fix: Menu board visible from at least 3 metres back in the queue.

The one thing most people don't think about until it's too late: Where does water go? Every commercial espresso machine needs a drain line. Every cleaning station needs a sink. Before you finalise bar placement, check where plumbing currently runs — moving it is expensive. The position of your drains often determines more of your bar layout than anything else.
